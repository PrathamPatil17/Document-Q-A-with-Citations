# OECD Recommendation of the Council on Artificial Intelligence (OECD/LEGAL/0449)

## Scope and Legal Status
This Recommendation on Artificial Intelligence was adopted by the OECD Council in 2019 and updated in 2024. As an OECD Recommendation, it is a legal instrument of the Organisation, but it is not legally binding. Recommendations represent a political commitment by adhering countries to the principles they contain and carry an expectation that adherents will do their utmost to implement them, but there is no enforcement mechanism and no penalty for non-implementation. The Recommendation has been adhered to by OECD member countries and a number of non-member economies, and it has been influential as a reference point for national AI strategies and for other international frameworks. Its force derives from shared political commitment and peer influence rather than from binding legal obligation.

## Value-Based Principles for Trustworthy AI
The Recommendation sets out five complementary value-based principles for the responsible stewardship of trustworthy AI. First, AI should benefit people and the planet by driving inclusive growth, sustainable development, and well-being. Second, AI systems should be designed in a way that respects the rule of law, human rights, democratic values, and diversity, and they should include appropriate safeguards — for example, enabling human intervention where necessary — to ensure a fair and just society. Third, there should be transparency and responsible disclosure around AI systems to ensure that people understand and can challenge AI-based outcomes. Fourth, AI systems must function in a robust, secure, and safe way throughout their life cycles, and potential risks should be continually assessed and managed. Fifth, organisations and individuals developing, deploying, or operating AI systems should be held accountable for their proper functioning in line with these principles.

## Human Rights and Democratic Values
The Recommendation places respect for human rights and democratic values at the centre of AI stewardship. AI actors should respect the rule of law, human rights, and democratic values throughout the AI system lifecycle. These include freedom, dignity, autonomy, privacy and data protection, non-discrimination and equality, diversity, fairness, social justice, and internationally recognised labour rights. To this end, AI actors should implement mechanisms and safeguards, such as capacity for human determination, that are appropriate to the context and consistent with the state of the art.

## Biometric and Surveillance Considerations
The Recommendation does not single out biometric identification or surveillance for specific prohibition. Instead, such technologies are addressed through the general principles: they should respect human rights, privacy, and democratic values, incorporate appropriate human oversight and safeguards, and be subject to transparency and accountability. The instrument leaves it to adhering governments to translate these principles into concrete national rules, whether permissive or restrictive. Because the Recommendation is non-binding, it expresses expectations about responsible use rather than imposing categorical bans on particular applications.

## Recommendations to Policy Makers
The Recommendation also provides recommendations to governments. Governments should invest in AI research and development, foster an inclusive and enabling digital ecosystem for AI, shape an interoperable governance and policy environment for AI, build human capacity and prepare for labour market transitions, and cooperate internationally for trustworthy AI. These provisions encourage international cooperation and interoperability of AI governance frameworks so that trustworthy AI can be developed and deployed across borders.
