# EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

## Subject Matter and Scope
This Regulation lays down harmonised rules on artificial intelligence across the European Union. It establishes a uniform legal framework for the development, the placing on the market, the putting into service, and the use of AI systems in the Union, in accordance with Union values. The Regulation applies to providers placing AI systems on the market or putting them into service in the Union, irrespective of whether those providers are established within the Union or in a third country. It also applies to deployers of AI systems located within the Union, and to providers and deployers located in a third country where the output produced by the AI system is used in the Union. The Regulation adopts a risk-based approach: obligations are calibrated to the level of risk an AI system poses, ranging from minimal risk, to limited risk, to high risk, and finally to an unacceptable risk category whose practices are prohibited outright. Military, defence, and national security uses fall outside the scope of this Regulation.

## Prohibited AI Practices
The following artificial intelligence practices are prohibited because they pose an unacceptable risk to fundamental rights and Union values. The placing on the market, putting into service, or use of an AI system that deploys subliminal techniques beyond a person's consciousness, or purposefully manipulative or deceptive techniques, with the objective or effect of materially distorting behaviour in a manner that causes significant harm, is prohibited. The use of AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities of a person or group due to their age, disability, or specific social or economic situation is prohibited. Social scoring of natural persons by public or private actors, where it leads to detrimental or unfavourable treatment, is prohibited.

Crucially, the use of real-time remote biometric identification systems in publicly accessible spaces for the purposes of law enforcement is prohibited, save for narrowly and exhaustively defined exceptions such as the targeted search for specific victims of abduction or a concrete and present terrorist threat. Any such exceptional use is subject to prior authorisation by a judicial or independent administrative authority. Untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet or CCTV footage to build facial recognition databases is prohibited. These prohibitions are legally binding and enforceable, and take effect ahead of most other obligations in the Regulation.

## Classification of High-Risk AI Systems
An AI system is classified as high-risk where it is intended to be used as a safety component of a product, or is itself a product, covered by specified Union harmonisation legislation. In addition, AI systems used in defined areas — including biometrics, critical infrastructure, education and vocational training, employment and worker management, access to essential private and public services, law enforcement, migration and border control, and the administration of justice — are classified as high-risk. High-risk classification triggers mandatory requirements including risk management systems, data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency, human oversight, and appropriate levels of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity. Conformity assessment must be completed before such a system is placed on the market.

## Compliance and Enforcement
Compliance with this Regulation is mandatory and legally binding. Non-compliance with the prohibitions on AI practices set out above shall be subject to administrative fines of up to 35,000,000 EUR or, if the offender is an undertaking, up to 7% of its total worldwide annual turnover for the preceding financial year, whichever is higher. Non-compliance with other obligations, including the requirements for high-risk AI systems, may be subject to administrative fines of up to 15,000,000 EUR or up to 3% of worldwide annual turnover. The supply of incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information to authorities may be subject to fines of up to 7,500,000 EUR or 1% of turnover. Each Member State shall designate national competent authorities for the purpose of supervision and enforcement, and a European Artificial Intelligence Office coordinates consistent application across the Union. These enforcement provisions make the Regulation directly binding on providers and deployers, without the need for national transposition.

## Transparency Obligations
Providers of AI systems intended to interact directly with natural persons shall ensure that those persons are informed that they are interacting with an AI system, unless this is obvious from the circumstances. Deployers of an AI system that generates or manipulates image, audio, or video content constituting a deep fake shall disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. Providers of general-purpose AI models are subject to specific transparency and documentation obligations, including maintaining technical documentation and a policy to comply with Union copyright law.
