# G7 Hiroshima Process International Guiding Principles for Advanced AI Systems (2023)

## Background and Legal Status
The International Guiding Principles for Organizations Developing Advanced AI Systems were agreed by G7 leaders in 2023 under the Hiroshima AI Process. They are accompanied by a voluntary Code of Conduct that provides more detailed guidance. The Guiding Principles are non-binding. They constitute a voluntary framework directed primarily at organizations developing the most advanced AI systems, including foundation models and generative AI, and they are intended to be a living document that will be reviewed and updated as technology evolves. There is no enforcement mechanism and no penalty attached; the Principles rely on voluntary commitment by organizations and on alignment with existing and emerging national frameworks. They are designed to complement, not replace, legally binding rules that jurisdictions may adopt.

## The Guiding Principles
The framework sets out a series of principles for organizations developing advanced AI systems. Organizations should take appropriate measures to identify, evaluate, and mitigate risks across the AI lifecycle, including through internal and independent external testing such as red-teaming. They should identify and mitigate vulnerabilities, and where appropriate, patterns of misuse after deployment, including through monitoring. Organizations should publicly report on the capabilities, limitations, and domains of appropriate and inappropriate use of advanced AI systems to support transparency and accountability. They should work towards responsible information sharing and reporting of incidents among organizations developing advanced AI systems, including with governments and civil society.

## Risk Management and Security
Organizations should invest in and implement robust security controls, including physical security, cybersecurity, and insider threat safeguards, to protect model weights and other sensitive assets across the AI lifecycle. They should develop and deploy reliable content authentication and provenance mechanisms, such as watermarking or other techniques, to enable users to identify AI-generated content. They should prioritize research to mitigate societal, safety, and security risks and prioritize the development of AI systems to address the world's greatest challenges, notably the climate crisis, global health, and education.

## Approach to Surveillance and Sensitive Uses
The Guiding Principles focus on the responsibilities of developers of advanced AI systems rather than on prohibiting specific downstream applications such as biometric identification or surveillance. They call for risk identification, mitigation, transparency, and the protection of privacy and intellectual property, but they do not impose categorical bans on particular use cases. Decisions on whether to restrict or prohibit specific applications are left to the binding laws of individual jurisdictions. As a voluntary instrument aimed at developers, the framework advances safety and accountability expectations without creating enforceable restrictions on how deployed systems may be used.

## Relationship to Binding Regulation
The Principles explicitly position themselves as complementary to legally binding regimes. G7 members recognized that different jurisdictions may take their own approaches, and that the voluntary Principles and Code of Conduct are meant to bridge the period before, and to operate alongside, binding regulation such as national legislation and regional frameworks. Organizations are encouraged to apply the Principles in a manner consistent with applicable law wherever they operate.
