# UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (2021)

## Scope and Legal Status
The Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence was adopted by the General Conference of UNESCO in November 2021, and represents the first global standard-setting instrument on the ethics of AI. It was adopted by all Member States of UNESCO. As a Recommendation, it is not a legally binding treaty; it does not create binding obligations under international law and carries no enforcement mechanism or sanction. Instead, Member States make a moral and political commitment to apply its provisions, to report periodically on the steps they have taken, and to promote its principles at national and international levels. Its aim is to provide a universal framework of values, principles, and actions to guide States and other actors in the responsible development and use of AI.

## Values and Principles
The Recommendation is founded on four core values: respect, protection, and promotion of human rights and fundamental freedoms and human dignity; environment and ecosystem flourishing; ensuring diversity and inclusiveness; and living in peaceful, just, and interconnected societies. From these values it derives principles including proportionality and do no harm, safety and security, fairness and non-discrimination, sustainability, right to privacy and data protection, human oversight and determination, transparency and explainability, responsibility and accountability, awareness and literacy, and multi-stakeholder and adaptive governance. The principle of proportionality requires that AI methods should not exceed what is necessary to achieve a legitimate aim, and that risk assessments should be used to prevent harms.

## Prohibition of Social Scoring and Mass Surveillance
The Recommendation takes a notably strong position on certain uses. It states that AI systems should not be used for social scoring or mass surveillance purposes. It affirms that Member States should ensure that AI systems are not used for social scoring or that would infringe upon human rights and fundamental freedoms, and it calls on States to adopt regulatory frameworks that give effect to this position. While the instrument itself is non-binding, it explicitly urges Member States to prohibit these applications through their own national law, expressing a clearer restrictive stance on mass surveillance and social scoring than most other non-binding international instruments.

## Human Oversight and Determination
The Recommendation affirms that Member States should ensure that it is always possible to attribute ethical and legal responsibility for any stage of the life cycle of AI systems to natural or legal persons. Human oversight and determination means that the ultimate responsibility and accountability must always lie with natural or legal persons, and that AI systems should not displace ultimate human responsibility. Life-and-death decisions should not be ceded to AI systems.

## Policy Action Areas
The Recommendation sets out eleven policy action areas through which Member States are encouraged to implement its principles, including ethical impact assessment, ethical governance and stewardship, data policy, development and international cooperation, environment and ecosystems, gender, culture, education and research, communication and information, economy and labour, and health and social well-being. Member States are encouraged to introduce ethical impact assessments and readiness assessment methodologies to translate the values and principles into concrete practice.
