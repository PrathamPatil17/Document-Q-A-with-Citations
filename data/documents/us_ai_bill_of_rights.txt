# Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights (White House OSTP, 2022)

## Purpose and Legal Status
The Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights is a set of five principles and associated practices published by the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy to help guide the design, use, and deployment of automated systems so that they protect the rights of the American public. The Blueprint is explicitly non-binding. It does not constitute United States government policy in the sense of a statute or regulation, it is not enforceable, and it does not create any legal right, benefit, or defence enforceable against the United States or any agency. It is intended as a framework and a statement of values to inform legislation, agency action, and industry practice, rather than as a legally operative instrument. Its influence depends on voluntary adoption and on its adoption into other, binding measures.

## Safe and Effective Systems
Members of the public should be protected from unsafe or ineffective systems. Automated systems should be developed with consultation from diverse communities, stakeholders, and domain experts to identify concerns, risks, and potential impacts of the system. Systems should undergo pre-deployment testing, risk identification and mitigation, and ongoing monitoring that demonstrate they are safe and effective based on their intended use. Outcomes of these protective measures should include the possibility of not deploying a system, or of removing a system from use, where safety and effectiveness cannot be assured.

## Algorithmic Discrimination Protections
People should not face discrimination by algorithms, and systems should be used and designed in an equitable way. Algorithmic discrimination occurs when automated systems contribute to unjustified different treatment or impacts disfavouring people on the basis of protected characteristics. Designers, developers, and deployers of automated systems should take proactive and continuous measures to protect individuals and communities from algorithmic discrimination and to use and design systems in an equitable way, including proactive equity assessments, use of representative data, and ongoing disparity testing.

## Data Privacy and Surveillance
The public should be protected from abusive data practices via built-in protections and should have agency over how data about them is used. Automated systems should seek permission and respect people's decisions regarding collection, use, access, transfer, and deletion of their data in appropriate ways. The Blueprint states that surveillance technologies should be subject to heightened oversight that includes at least pre-deployment assessment of their potential harms and scope limits to protect privacy and civil liberties. Continuous surveillance and monitoring should not be used in ways that limit access to critical resources or services, or that undermine the ability to exercise rights. However, the Blueprint does not itself prohibit any particular biometric or surveillance technology; it recommends heightened oversight and scope limits rather than imposing an enforceable ban.

## Notice, Explanation, and Human Alternatives
People should know that an automated system is being used and understand how and why it contributes to outcomes that affect them. Designers and deployers should provide generally accessible plain-language documentation including clear descriptions of the overall system functioning and the role automation plays. Furthermore, people should be able to opt out, where appropriate, and have access to a person who can quickly consider and remedy problems they encounter. Where an automated system fails, produces an error, or a person wishes to appeal or contest its impacts, a human fallback and escalation process should be available.
