# NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)

## Purpose and Audience
The AI Risk Management Framework is intended for voluntary use to improve the ability of organizations to incorporate trustworthiness considerations into the design, development, use, and evaluation of AI products, services, and systems. Developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology through a consensus-driven, open, and collaborative process, the Framework is a resource for organizations designing, developing, deploying, or using AI systems to help manage the many risks of AI and promote trustworthy and responsible development. It is intended to be practical, to adapt to the AI landscape as technologies continue to develop, and to be operationalized by organizations of all sizes and in all sectors.

## Voluntary and Non-Binding Nature
The Framework is voluntary. It does not impose legal obligations, and it is not a regulation. It confers no rights and imposes no requirements. Rather, it offers guidance that organizations may adopt in whole or in part according to their needs, risk tolerance, and resources. Unlike a binding legal instrument, the Framework does not prescribe penalties for non-adoption, and there is no enforcement authority attached to it. Its influence is intended to come from its usefulness and from broad, voluntary uptake across industry, academia, civil society, and government, rather than from legal compulsion. Organizations retain discretion over how, and whether, to apply its recommendations.

## The Four Core Functions
The Framework organizes AI risk management activities into four core functions. The GOVERN function cultivates a culture of risk management within organizations, establishing policies, processes, and accountability structures. The MAP function establishes the context to frame risks related to an AI system, including its purpose, its stakeholders, and its potential impacts. The MEASURE function employs quantitative, qualitative, or mixed-method tools to analyze, assess, benchmark, and monitor AI risk and related impacts. The MANAGE function allocates resources to mapped and measured risks on a regular basis and as defined by the GOVERN function, including responding to, recovering from, and communicating about incidents. Together these functions are intended to be performed iteratively across the AI lifecycle.

## Characteristics of Trustworthy AI
The Framework identifies characteristics of trustworthy AI systems that organizations should weigh and balance according to context. Trustworthy AI is described as valid and reliable, safe, secure and resilient, accountable and transparent, explainable and interpretable, privacy-enhanced, and fair with harmful bias managed. The Framework stresses that these characteristics often involve trade-offs; for example, enhancing interpretability may affect accuracy, and optimizing for one property may come at the expense of another. Balancing these characteristics is a contextual decision that depends on the values and priorities of the deploying organization and the affected communities.

## Managing Biometric and Sensitive Data Risks
The Framework treats biometric data and other sensitive data as sources of heightened risk to be managed through the core functions rather than through categorical prohibition. Organizations using biometric technologies such as facial recognition are encouraged to map the context of use, measure risks including the potential for demographic bias and privacy harms, and manage those risks with appropriate governance, testing, and human oversight. The Framework does not ban any specific application of biometric identification; instead it provides a structured, voluntary process through which an organization can decide whether and how to deploy such a system responsibly. Decisions about acceptable use, including whether a given deployment is appropriate at all, remain with the organization applying the Framework.

## Relationship to Other Efforts
The Framework is designed to be used alongside, and to complement, existing laws, regulations, standards, and organizational practices. It is intended to be interoperable with international approaches to AI risk management and to evolve through periodic updates and the development of a companion Playbook and profiles tailored to particular sectors and use cases.
